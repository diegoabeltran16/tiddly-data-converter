# Technical Report: Tiddler System
## Introduction
The structured tiddler system, exportable as JSON and static HTML, is conceived as a non-linear knowledge infrastructure designed to study, trace, and develop a topic in a disciplined yet flexible way. It is intended to serve multiple purposes simultaneously, including human learning, computational analysis, knowledge graph construction, integration into data pipelines, and use in AI contexts. Its operation rests on a central distinction between two complementary levels: on one hand, the tiddler as a unit of human work within TiddlyWiki, where knowledge can emerge in an exploratory, progressive, and still-incomplete way; on the other, the tiddler as a structured unit that, after passing through the converter, is integrated into a computationally interpretable canon suitable for analysis, auditing, and systematic reuse. Thus, the model is governed by a single guiding principle: **the human edits a semantically sufficient interface —TiddlyWiki—; the converter formalizes the canonical representation**. This distinction creates a controlled transition between human meaning-making and the technical formalization required for systematic reuse, without sacrificing either the richness of learning or the system's structural integrity.

The importance of this model today lies in addressing an increasingly evident need: to produce knowledge that can be understood by people and, at the same time, traced, validated, and reused by computational systems without losing its epistemological context. In an environment marked by information overload, collaboration with artificial intelligence, growing complexity, and demands for reproducibility, efficient traceability, and coherent semantic relationships, accumulating isolated notes or unstructured documents is insufficient. What is required instead is an architecture that allows distinguishing between fact, hypothesis, interpretation, and procedure; recording provenance, levels of certainty, and semantic relations; and preserving a balance between intellectual exploration and formal discipline. Under this logic, the tiddler system does not function merely as an information repository, but as a platform for the construction, organization, and auditable evolution of knowledge across multiple domains.

This architectural logic finds empirical and theoretical grounding in recent related work spanning semantic note systems, knowledge graph lifecycle governance, provenance management, personal knowledge graphs, semantic annotation, and layered learning architectures. A task-based evaluation of SemNotes by Drăgan et al. found that interlinked notes delivered results that were "significantly faster" on complex retrieval tasks with "little or no extra effort" for annotation, supporting the premise that explicit semantic interlinking improves retrieval without imposing disproportionate capture costs [1]. At the organizational scale, Geisler et al. formalize knowledge graph ecosystems as structured tuples governed through explicit lifecycles, arguing that reusable knowledge infrastructure requires controlled state transitions rather than ad hoc accumulation [2]. Complementary provenance research by Kleinsteuber et al. [3] and Samuel et al. [4] demonstrates that traceable provenance —co-located with knowledge artifacts and supporting reproducibility and bidirectional convertibility— is essential for maintaining trust in evolving knowledge bases. From the personal knowledge management perspective, Skjæveland et al. define a personal knowledge graph by owner control and argue that the representation layer must handle a "rich set of metadata and context data" extending well beyond the primary assertions, noting that "provenance data is also vital" for synchronization with private data sources [5]. Uren et al. provide a foundational analysis of why semantic document infrastructure requires formal coordination between content, vocabulary, and annotation rather than informal markup [6]. At the architectural level, Behrouz et al.'s Nested Learning paradigm formalizes multi-level systems in which each layer carries its own "context flow" and update frequency, providing an external vocabulary for this system's organizing principle: distinguishable levels with distinct informational roles and rhythms, designed for continual and revisable learning [7]. Each of these contributions is mapped in detail against the system's architecture in the alignment sections that follow, where correspondences and principled scope divergences are traced component by component.

### Structural Discipline and Intellectual Exploration
The system's architecture rests on a constitutive tension between two equally legitimate needs: structural discipline and intellectual exploration. The former is indispensable to ensure coherence, traceability, relational integrity, and compatibility with computational processes. Thanks to it, each tiddler can retain a stable identity, a recognizable minimum structure, and a network of explicit relations that enable validation, navigation, and subsequent processing. Without this normative dimension, the system would lose technical consistency and cease to be reliable as a basis for knowledge graphs, data pipelines, or AI applications. In this sense, structural discipline does not act as an external obstacle to learning, but as the condition that allows generated knowledge to preserve form, continuity, and reusability. This constitutive tension corresponds to what Behrouz et al. describe in the Nested Learning paradigm as the structural condition for maintaining both expressiveness and retention across update cycles: a system whose capacity depends on the interaction between distinguishable levels rather than on collapsing them into a single flat substrate [7].

However, the system was not designed solely to preserve already-stabilized knowledge; it was also designed to allow its progressive formation. For this reason, intellectual exploration occupies an equally central place. During the human editing phase, tiddlers may contain intuitions, open questions, preliminary hypotheses, and still-incomplete relations, without this invalidating their value within the learning process. This openness prevents the system from becoming a bureaucratic structure incapable of accompanying critical, creative, and intuitive thinking. The balance between both dimensions —discipline and exploration— therefore constitutes the model's organizing principle: the former ensures that knowledge can be audited and computed; the latter guarantees that the system remains a fertile environment for discovering, formulating, and refining ideas before their consolidation.

## Architectural alignment with related work

### Interlinking and contextual retrieval [1]
A task-based evaluation comparing SemNotes —a semantic note-taking tool for the Nepomuk-KDE Semantic Desktop— with Evernote found that interlinked notes improved performance on complex contextual retrieval tasks, delivering results that were "significantly faster" in one of the two most demanding search tasks tested [1]. The same study reported that adding links to notes did not produce a statistically significant increase in time spent on note creation despite the additional annotation step required, a finding the authors summarize as requiring "little or no extra effort" [1]. Participant feedback further supported the interlinked approach: 60% of participants reported that SemNotes helped them finish tasks faster, 80% said it helped them perform tasks better, and the tool received an overall impression rating of 4.15 out of 5. The evaluation drew on 20 participants and a realistic mixed dataset —130 contacts, 655 emails, 20 scientific papers, event photos, and 50 notes interlinked across people, projects, events, and other notes— which lends the findings a degree of practical credibility beyond purely theoretical claims. To maintain honest scope, the participant pool came from a Semantic Web research institute, which may have favoured semantic tools in perception-based responses; the authors also note, however, that none of the participants used Linux as their main operating system and several already used Evernote, factors that could have introduced a countervailing bias toward the baseline tool. While this evidence does not validate the Tiddler System directly, it supports the plausibility of one of its key premises: explicit semantic interlinking can make knowledge easier to re-find and navigate without imposing disproportionate costs on knowledge capture —precisely the balance the present architecture is designed to preserve.

Mapping the present system against SemNotes reveals that the two designs share a foundational architecture rooted in the lifecycle model Drăgan et al. adapt from the Abstract Data Life-Cycle: creation, refinement, access, termination, and publication. Each phase corresponds to a distinct operational layer in the Tiddler System. The creation phase —generating a new resource or importing existing data— maps to the act of authoring a new tiddler in TiddlyWiki and, in the automated pathway, to the converter ingesting a TiddlyWiki JSON export or session tiddler to produce a new canonical JSONL node complete with a UUID v5 identifier assigned at generation time. The refinement phase —modifying existing data and creating or deleting links between resources— maps directly to the human editing TiddlyWiki between sessions and to the re-run of the converter, which captures the delta by extending the `meta.status_history`, `meta.title_history`, and `metacognition_history` arrays with a new timestamped entry rather than overwriting the prior state; relation creation and deletion correspond to modifying the `relations` array, where each entry is a typed, directed edge with a target UUID rather than a free-text mention. The access phase —querying and browsing, including recursive traversal of the semantic subgraph reachable from a given resource— maps to two surfaces: direct reading of the Canon JSONL for computational consumers —who can follow UUID-based relation targets to traverse the relational graph— and TiddlyWiki's native link navigation for the human author. The termination phase —deleting a resource and determining how much of its connected subgraph to cascade— is handled in the present system through the `replaces` relation type, which records that a node supersedes a prior concept, and through the converter's collision-detection logic, which prevents orphaned UUID references from entering the Canon silently. The publication phase —exporting data to other formats and applications— corresponds directly to the Canon JSONL as the exportable, format-stable representation, and to the static HTML export from TiddlyWiki as the human-readable publication surface. The key architectural divergence between SemNotes and the present system lies in the interlinking philosophy: SemNotes constructs a single generic `pimo:isRelated` relation and explicitly defers more specific relations to future versions, basing this decision on Sauermann's long-term study finding that users prefer simpler properties even at the cost of semantic precision; the present system makes the opposite choice, treating typed relation categories as a core fidelity requirement from the outset, on the ground that the system's role as a personal knowledge graph —designed to feed AI, statistical, and BI pipelines— requires relational type information that a generic relatedness link cannot provide to downstream consumers. A second, more subtle divergence appears in the annotation unit: SemNotes links notes to external desktop resources —persons, projects, events, organizations— through entity recognition of named entities in note text; the present system does not perform automated entity recognition from free prose, instead requiring the human author to assert all relational claims explicitly through structured TiddlyWiki fields, preserving the author as the sole semantic authority over what counts as a relation and what its type is. This is not a capability deficit but a deliberate epistemological boundary: automated suggestion trades recall breadth for precision and risks introducing provenance-free links; the present system trades suggestion convenience for provenance-complete, human-authorized link records. The empirical finding that matters most for the present system's justification is the one Drăgan et al. treat as their main result: that the annotation overhead —measured in time— is not statistically significant even when the human creates explicit links, whereas the retrieval benefit for contextually complex tasks is statistically significant. This asymmetry between annotation cost and retrieval gain is exactly the economic assumption that underlies the present system's design philosophy: investing structured authoring effort at creation time yields compound returns in the form of navigable, typeable, provenance-traceable relational knowledge that unstructured note collections cannot provide.

### Knowledge graph ecosystem governance [2]
Recent work on knowledge graph ecosystems reinforces the view that robust knowledge systems should be managed not merely as static repositories, but as structured ecosystems governed through explicit life cycles. Geisler et al. formalize a knowledge graph ecosystem as a tuple comprising data sources, ontology, mappings, constraints, the graph itself, and a log —treating traceability and auditability as structural components rather than optional metadata [2]. Their insistence that "mature technical and organizational processes" are decisive for maintaining KGs is operationalized through a lifecycle model of ordered transformations, each step extending the log with inputs, outputs, timestamps, and validation results —a perspective that directly supports the architectural premise of the present system: reusable knowledge infrastructure requires controlled lifecycle management and traceable state transitions rather than ad hoc accumulation. While the paper addresses large-scale organizational KG ecosystems rather than personal knowledge tools, its six formal components map with direct structural correspondence onto the Tiddler System's architecture, as the following mapping makes explicit.

Mapping the present system against the six formal components of the KGE tuple reveals both the precision of the structural correspondence and the principled limits of its scope. The data sources component —a set with defined schema and instances— corresponds to two distinct sources in the present system: the TiddlyWiki export JSON, whose schema is determined by TiddlyWiki's native field structure, and the session tiddler, which provides structured session-level context; neither is a relational database or distributed federated source, but both carry schema-conformant instances that the converter processes. The ontology component —a logical theory defining entities and relationships through a structured vocabulary— corresponds to the present system's controlled semantic vocabulary: the enumerated tiddler types, the defined relation types, the controlled tagsets, and the epistemic marker set collectively constitute a single-author domain ontology that is not expressed in OWL or RDFS but satisfies the functional requirement of providing a shared conceptualization against which all nodes are validated. The mappings component —assertions linking ontology to data sources— corresponds directly to the converter's field-mapping rules: each converter rule transforms a named TiddlyWiki field into a named Canon JSONL field according to type inference logic, synonym normalization, and tag canonicalization tables; these rules are the mapping assertions that mediate between the human-editable surface and the formal ontological vocabulary. The constraints component —a logical theory ensuring consistency, accuracy, and quality— corresponds to the converter's schema validator: required fields, type constraints, enumeration bounds, relational integrity rules, and UUID collision detection are all expressed as explicit constraints that the converter enforces at generation time. The knowledge graph component —the rendering of the ontology with individuals from source data— corresponds to the canonical JSONL, which is precisely the rendering of the controlled vocabulary with individual tiddler instances derived from TiddlyWiki source data via the mapping rules. The log component —an ordered list with timestamps, before/after states, and change descriptions— corresponds to the three per-node history arrays: `meta.title_history`, `meta.status_history`, and `metacognition_history` collectively record the succession of states across edit sessions with timestamps, making the evolution of each node independently auditable without a centralized change log. The five actor roles Geisler et al. define —Knowledge Provider, Builder, Auditor, Analyst, Consumer— collapse in the single-author context: the human is simultaneously provider (defining requirements and domain knowledge), builder (editing tiddlers and importing session artifacts), auditor (reviewing epistemic states and relational consistency), analyst (querying the Canon for patterns and insights), and consumer (reading the structured knowledge back through TiddlyWiki). This role collapse is not a deficiency of the present system but a deliberate boundary condition: the architecture is designed for single-author knowledge work where role separation would add overhead without governance benefit, and the `epistemic_state` and `metacognition` fields serve the self-auditing function that the Knowledge Auditor role would otherwise fill in a multi-actor setting. The three scope divergences are equally clear: the paper targets distributed multi-organization ecosystems requiring interoperability across different ontologies, heterogeneous data formats, and federated access controls; the present system targets a single-author local environment where interoperability is solved by uniform schema rather than cross-ontology alignment, and where access control is filesystem-level ownership rather than role-based policy. The paper's lifecycle steps —creation, ontology evolution, validation, querying, monitoring, and optimization— find analogs in the converter pipeline, but the present system does not implement a query layer, a monitoring service, or an automated optimization process; those lifecycle phases are handled by the human author reading and revising TiddlyWiki directly rather than through computational mediation.

### Provenance management in knowledge graph platforms [3]
Kleinsteuber et al. argue that "proper provenance management" is essential for understanding data origins, increasing trust, supporting open science principles, and enabling "reproducibility and updatability" [3]. Their framework treats provenance not as an afterthought attached to final outputs, but as workflow-level information that must be captured, stored, and retrieved: it records executed tools, configurations, and input/output objects across each phase of KG generation, and shows that such structured provenance can support four concrete capabilities —reproducibility, altered rerun, undo operations, and provenance retrieval. This maps directly onto the design logic of the present system, where the converter does not merely produce a canonical output but formalizes the conditions and pathway of that production —a PROV-DM correspondence the following mapping traces in detail.

Mapping the present system's architecture against the Kleinsteuber et al. provenance framework reveals a structured correspondence with the paper's foundational PROV-DM model while diverging in scope along the dimensions of multi-user workflow orchestration and formal query infrastructure. The paper's mapping of the KG generation process onto PROV-DM concepts maps directly onto the Tiddler System's architectural components: the human editor corresponds to the paper's `prov:Agent`; each converter run corresponds to a `prov:Activity`; the source tiddler JSON export corresponds to the input `prov:Entity`; the canonical JSONL node corresponds to the generated output entity; and the provenance block embedded in each node encodes the derivation chain that corresponds to `prov:wasDerivedFrom`. The paper identifies the workflow —an ordered collection of tool executions— as a `prov:Plan`; the session tiddler in the present system serves a functionally equivalent role, recording the structured plan governing a knowledge work session that the converter then formalizes into canonical entities. The paper's distinction between two storage approaches is also architecturally meaningful here: the system adopts the equivalent of Approach 1 —co-locating provenance with the knowledge artifact itself rather than in a separate relational database— on the ground that any consumer of the Canon JSONL receives provenance co-resident with content without requiring an auxiliary join, exactly the "smooth integration" advantage the paper identifies for triple-based co-location; the trade-off that approach entails —that storing provenance inside the artifact increases the size of the records— is precisely the dynamic the present system validates empirically, where provenance, meta, and metacognition blocks routinely exceed the content field in byte count. The four applications the paper identifies map onto capabilities the present system addresses by different mechanisms and at a different granularity: reproducibility corresponds to the ability to re-run the converter over the same TiddlyWiki source to regenerate the Canon with identical UUID v5 identifiers and preserved derivation records; altered rerun corresponds to exporting a revised TiddlyWiki state and regenerating the Canon in which the delta is captured in `meta.status_history`, `meta.title_history`, and `metacognition_history`; the undo operation finds its structural analog in those same history arrays, which function as an append-only audit log from which prior states can be reconstructed, though no automated rollback mechanism exists; and provenance retrieval corresponds to direct inspection of the `provenance` block co-resident in each canonical node, immediately accessible without a query layer. The principal scope divergence is clear: the Kleinsteuber et al. framework addresses multi-user web portal environments where tool execution pipelines must be orchestrated, secured, monitored, and re-invoked at the phase level by multiple agents with differentiated roles; the present system addresses single-author local workflows where the converter is the sole formalization tool, provenance is captured at transformation time rather than across a configurable pipeline, and the absence of a web infrastructure eliminates the security, dependency isolation, and backend orchestration requirements that motivate much of the paper's engineering complexity. A second, equally important divergence concerns granularity: the paper explicitly describes its framework as coarse-grained provenance at the workflow and phase level, and acknowledges that tracking how each individual input entry was transformed at each step would require finer granularity not yet provided. The present system inverts this priority —it captures fine-grained semantic provenance at the node and field level, recording individual `epistemic_state`, `certainty`, `ambiguity`, and content values across successive edit sessions— while not tracking the computational pipeline provenance of the converter execution itself. These are complementary rather than conflicting granularity choices: the paper optimizes for tracing which tools ran on which data in which order; the present system optimizes for tracing what was known, with what confidence, and on the basis of what source —a division that reflects the fundamentally different nature of the epistemic objects each system is designed to manage.

### Provenance co-location and bidirectional convertibility [4]
Samuel et al. present ProvBook, an extension that captures and visualizes provenance over time in Jupyter notebooks, on the ground that interactive modification of computational artifacts requires understanding how results changed across runs to support "trust and reproducibility" [4]. Their implementation embeds provenance directly in the metadata of each editable cell rather than in a separate log, permits users to compare prior and current states through a timeline slider, and supports bidirectional conversion between notebooks and RDF —including the ability to "convert it back to a notebook"— using PROV-O and the REPRODUCE-ME ontology. While the paper is situated strictly within computational notebook environments and tracks cell executions rather than semantic content evolution, its two governing architectural principles —provenance co-location with editable artifacts and round-trip convertibility between human and machine representations— are directly applicable to the present system, as the following mapping demonstrates.

Mapping the present system against ProvBook's design reveals a concentrated alignment on two architectural decisions that the paper treats as foundational and the present system enacts through structurally distinct but functionally equivalent mechanisms. The first is the principle of provenance co-location: ProvBook stores provenance data inside the metadata of each editable cell rather than in a separate log, on the ground that this keeps the record physically bound to the artifact it describes and makes it transportable alongside rather than separately from the content. The present system operationalizes the identical principle at the level of knowledge content rather than code execution: the `provenance` block is embedded directly inside each canonical JSONL node, co-resident with the semantic content it traces, so that any export, transfer, or downstream ingestion of the Canon necessarily carries its provenance record without requiring a join against an external store. The second alignment is on bidirectional convertibility: ProvBook's capacity to convert a notebook to RDF and then "convert it back to a notebook" treats parse-and-regenerate round-trip fidelity as a non-negotiable design requirement rather than an optional export feature; the present system's bidirectional Canon imposes the same constraint between TiddlyWiki and JSONL, ensuring that edits made through the canonical form can be reflected back to the human-editable surface without structural loss. A third, more granular correspondence exists at the level of temporal state comparison: ProvBook's timeline slider allows users to navigate cell execution history and compare prior and current outputs; the `metacognition_history`, `meta.status_history`, and `meta.title_history` arrays of the present system serve the analogous function for semantic content —recording not when a cell was run but what was believed, at what certainty level, and under what label across successive edit sessions, making the epistemic trajectory of a knowledge claim as navigable as ProvBook makes the computational trajectory of a code cell. The structural decomposition also maps with precision: ProvBook models the notebook as a `p-plan:Plan` and each cell as a `p-plan:Step`, with each execution as a `CellExecution` subclassing `p-plan:Activity`; the present system achieves a functionally equivalent decomposition by treating the session tiddler as the structured plan governing a knowledge work session, individual tiddlers as the minimal units of semantic claim, and each converter run as the formalization activity that transforms human-editable artifacts into canonical entities. The scope divergence is clear and principled: ProvBook tracks the execution provenance of computational artifacts —what code ran, when, how long it took, what output it produced— while the present system tracks the semantic evolution of knowledge claims —what was asserted, with what certainty, attributed to what source, and how that assessment changed over time. ProvBook operates in multi-user JupyterHub environments with SPARQL querying capability over RDF execution traces; the present system operates in a single-author local environment with no query endpoint, exchanging RDF alignment for broad JSON tooling compatibility. These are not equivalent architectures for equivalent use cases; they are architectures that share two governing principles —provenance co-location and round-trip convertibility— while addressing fundamentally different epistemic objects: executable scientific computations versus structured human knowledge claims.

### Personal knowledge graph ecosystem [5]
Skjæveland et al. argue that a holistic view is necessary to unlock the full potential of a personal knowledge system, proposing a unified PKG ecosystem organized around three main aspects —population, representation and management, and utilization— and emphasizing that a PKG only reaches its potential when it is situated within clear interfaces toward data sources, ontologies, management services, and downstream consuming services [5]. They define a personal knowledge graph not by its content but by ownership: a single individual holds full read and write access and the exclusive right to grant others access, with the primary purpose of enabling services customized to that owner. This owner-control criterion distinguishes a genuine personal knowledge graph from a merely personalized graph built by a service about a user without that user's administrative authority —a distinction directly applicable to the present system, whose guiding principle places the human as the semantic authority over the knowledge base rather than as a passive subject of profiling. The paper further argues that the representation and management dimension must handle not only facts but also a "rich set of metadata and context data" capturing where, when, how, and by whom a statement came about, and to what degree it is held to be true; it notes that "provenance data is also vital" for synchronization with private data sources and suggests that the metadata will greatly exceed the actual data in size. This aligns precisely with the architecture of the present system, where provenance blocks, status histories, title histories, epistemic states, and relational records are not optional annotations but core structural components of each canonical node. The scope of this work is a conceptual survey and roadmap rather than an empirical study or implemented prototype; its value for the present report lies in its ecosystem framing and ownership-centered definition rather than in measured performance claims.

Mapping the present system against the three aspects of the PKG ecosystem framework reveals a structured pattern of alignment and principled scope. The population aspect —which covers adding data from private and public sources and synchronizing changes back to those sources— is addressed within a deliberately single-source architecture: TiddlyWiki constitutes the system's private data source, the converter performs the population step by transforming tiddler exports into the Canon JSONL, and the bidirectional Canon provides the synchronization pathway back to the editable surface. Population from public data sources, external knowledge graphs such as Wikidata, or unstructured external corpora is outside the current scope; this boundary reflects a deliberate prioritization of authorship depth and provenance fidelity over ingestion breadth. The representation and management aspect is where the alignment is most precise: the schema's `epistemic_state` field, `metacognition.certainty` and `metacognition.ambiguity` scores, `provenance` block, and three separate history arrays implement directly the rich set of metadata and context data the paper identifies as necessary —and the system empirically validates the paper's prediction that metadata will greatly exceed the actual data in size, since the provenance, meta, and metacognition blocks of a typical canonical node routinely exceed the content field in byte count. The management gap is equally real: the system has no query interface, no SPARQL endpoint, and no formal access-grant mechanism; access to the Canon is controlled at the filesystem level by the owner, not through a service layer with selective read and write permissions for external users. The utilization aspect is addressed structurally rather than operationally: the `meta.intended_use` field —which classifies each node as intended for business intelligence, AI, statistical, Markov, or general consumption— pre-categorizes downstream use at authoring time, and the Canon format is designed for AI pipelines, statistical analysis, and knowledge graph tooling, but services must read the Canon file directly rather than querying a managed endpoint. The ownership criterion is the cleanest correspondence: TiddlyWiki's single-file, locally-stored architecture makes owner control inherent by construction rather than enforced by policy, and the system's guiding principle —that the human is the semantic authority who edits a sufficient interface while the converter formalizes the canonical representation— gives direct architectural instantiation to the paper's definition of a genuine personal knowledge graph as distinct from a system built about a user rather than by and for that user.

### Semantic annotation in knowledge management [6]
A foundational justification for treating structured notes as semantic knowledge assets rather than inert files comes from earlier work on semantic annotation in document-centric knowledge management. Uren et al. observe that much organizational knowledge resides in documents, yet conventional content management systems offer limited support for structuring or interpreting them; they argue that ontology-based semantic annotation can address this gap by transforming ordinary documents into "intelligent" resources that know about their own content, enabling automated processes to support "enhanced information retrieval" and improved interoperability [6]. Their document-centric model treats documents, ontologies, and annotations as three tightly coupled data classes requiring strong coupling between search tools, annotation tools, and ontology-maintenance tools to handle document reuse, ontology evolution, and access control. They further specify seven technical requirements for viable semantic annotation systems in knowledge management contexts: standard formats, user-centered and collaborative design, support for multiple and evolving ontologies, heterogeneous document formats, consistency across document evolution, appropriate annotation storage, and automation. Two of these requirements —ontology evolution and annotation storage— map directly onto core design decisions in the present system, a correspondence the detailed mapping that follows makes explicit. The paper is a 2006 survey and requirements analysis rather than an empirical benchmark study; its contribution to the present report is conceptual and normative —providing a well-articulated framework for why semantic document infrastructure requires formal coordination between content, vocabulary, and annotation rather than informal or ad hoc markup.

Mapping the present system's architecture against these seven requirements reveals a pattern of substantial alignment alongside two principled trade-offs. The requirements for document evolution and annotation consistency (R5) and for appropriate annotation storage (R6) are the most fully met: the provenance block, title histories, status histories, and metacognition histories embedded in each canonical node ensure that every change to a tiddler leaves a traceable record, while the architectural separation between human-editable TiddlyWiki and the canonical JSONL layer implements precisely the separate-storage model Uren et al. argue is superior for reuse, centralized querying, and role-sensitive access —with the bidirectional converter resolving the main practical objection to separate storage by preserving the ability to reconstruct the editable surface from the Canon. The user-centered design requirement (R2) is strongly satisfied at the single-owner level: the decision that the human should never write canonical JSON, and that TiddlyWiki should serve as the cognitive working surface while the converter handles serialization, is a deliberate ergonomic commitment of precisely the kind the paper advocates; the gap is that multi-user collaboration and role-differentiated write access across multiple contributors are not addressed in the current architecture. The automation requirement (R7) is met at the level of formalization: the converter automates UUID v5 generation, tag canonicalization and collision detection, schema validation, content-type inference, history tracking, and canonical projection, though it does not attempt semantic extraction from raw unstructured text —that semantic work is intentionally assigned to the human author as the system's semantic authority. Two requirements are met in spirit rather than fully in letter: the standard formats requirement (R1) is addressed through a versioned, open JSON schema rather than the RDF/OWL stack Uren et al. associate with Semantic Web interoperability, gaining broad tooling support at the cost of native graph query capability; and the ontology support requirement (R3) is well covered for vocabulary evolution —through synonym maps, controlled tag migration, and the `replaces` relation type— but the system operates with a single controlled vocabulary rather than multiple composable or externally linked ontologies. The requirement that receives the least coverage is heterogeneous document formats (R4): the converter's input is TiddlyWiki exclusively, not word-processor files, PDFs, spreadsheets, or multimedia. This is a principled boundary rather than an oversight; the system gains depth of semantic structure, provenance fidelity, and cognitive ergonomics by committing to one authoritative source environment rather than attempting to annotate across a heterogeneous document landscape.

### Layered architecture and Nested Learning [7]
A more fundamental architectural rationale for the system's non-linear, layered organization is offered by recent work on Nested Learning. Behrouz et al. propose a learning paradigm in which any machine learning model and its associated optimization process can be coherently represented as a set of nested, multi-level, and/or parallel problems, each carrying its own "context flow" — the internal informational substrate that a given level processes and transforms [7]. Operating under this paradigm, the authors argue that conventional deep learning methods learn by compressing their own context flow, that in-context learning emerges naturally at sufficient scale as a structural consequence of hierarchical organization rather than as a separately engineered feature, and that systems possessing more levels are capable of "higher-order in-context learning" and of approaching genuine continual learning — the capacity to progressively incorporate new knowledge without catastrophically displacing prior knowledge. The paper further formalizes the notion of update frequency as a property of each system component: components at different levels may legitimately operate at different rhythms, from fast sensory integration to slow deep consolidation, a distinction the authors ground in neurophysiological oscillation theory and map onto architectural form. While Nested Learning is situated entirely within machine learning research and does not address personal knowledge management, semantic notebooks, or TiddlyWiki, its conceptual framework provides a precise external vocabulary for the architectural logic already embedded in the present system: that a knowledge environment organized as multiple distinguishable levels, each with its own informational role and update cadence, is not an ad hoc complication but a principled structural response to the epistemic demands of continual, revisable, and computationally tractable learning.

Mapping the present system against the Nested Learning paradigm reveals a structural analogy that clarifies why the system's layered architecture is neither decorative nor incidental. Behrouz et al. define a nested system as an ordered arrangement of levels in which each level carries its own context flow, its own update frequency, and its own optimization objective, and they argue that the expressiveness and continual learning capacity of the whole depend on the interaction between levels rather than on any single level's properties in isolation. Against this framework, the Tiddler System exhibits a clear four-level organization: the stable mother-nuclei layer, which changes infrequently and anchors the semantic vocabulary; the thematic deployment layer, comprising canonical topic tiddlers that evolve as understanding develops; the dynamic session layer, whose nodes are generated rapidly and may be later promoted, replaced, or retired; and the converter-managed formalization layer, which operates as an independent process that reads the human-editable surface and produces the canonical JSONL representation with its own validation rules, UUID assignment, history tracking, and schema enforcement. These four levels do not process the same kind of content at the same rhythm — the mother-nuclei update on the timescale of reconceptualization, the thematic layer on the timescale of study cycles, the session layer on the timescale of individual work sessions, and the converter layer on the timescale of individual conversion runs — which is precisely the multi-frequency structure that Behrouz et al. identify as enabling both expressive learning and retention across update cycles. The paper's rejection of binary short-term/long-term memory in favor of a continuum of update frequencies and persistence levels is also reflected in the Tiddler System's node taxonomy: a session-born tiddler may begin as exploratory, carry an `epistemic_state` of 'hypothesis,' be promoted to a canonical thematic node, and eventually become stable enough to serve as a reference anchor for new session nodes — a lifecycle trajectory that does not fit a flat storage model and that requires the layered, provenance-complete architecture the system provides. The single principled divergence between the NL framework and the present system is one of scope and mechanism: Nested Learning addresses gradient-based parameter optimization in machine learning, while the present system manages human-authored semantic content; the "optimization" in this system is epistemic rather than parametric, enacted through human revision, converter normalization, and structural enforcement rather than backpropagation. This divergence does not undermine the analogy — it frames it correctly as an architectural parallel rather than a computational identity, and situates the Tiddler System as a design that draws on the same structural principle without overclaiming technical equivalence.

## Structure and Classification of System Rules

Before formalizing the rules, it is necessary to distinguish between the minimum node structure in the human phase and its subsequent formalization in the converted phase, since this difference conditions the type of normative requirement applicable at each stage of the system.

### Minimum Node Structure: Human Phase and Converted Phase
The minimum node structure should be understood at two complementary levels, corresponding to two distinct moments in the system. The first is the tiddler in the human phase: the unit of knowledge as it is created, edited, and refined within TiddlyWiki. At this stage, the node functions primarily as a cognitive and epistemic instrument: it allows recording ideas, concepts, hypotheses, initial relations, and content in formation, preserving both meaning and the state of knowledge. Its purpose is not to immediately maximize computational exploitation, but to facilitate human meaning-making within a flexible, traceable, and semantically oriented environment. For this reason, although the human tiddler must respect a minimum structure, that structure should focus on what the human actually governs: semantic content, relations, epistemic state, provenance, and metacognition. Data such as hashes, timestamps, technical content-type detection, indexes, derived profiles, and other pipeline-specific information do not belong to the human-editable core, but to the converter layer.

The second level corresponds to the node in the converted phase: the structured unit that results once the complete TiddlyWiki passes through the converter (`tiddly-data-converter`). In this phase, the tiddler ceases to operate solely as a thinking tool and becomes part of an auditable, deterministic, and reversible JSONL Canon. The converter does not replace human content; it formalizes, normalizes, and complements it with the technical data required for systematic use in contexts such as JSONL, statistical analysis, knowledge graphs, AI, or controlled reverse processes. It is important to emphasize that the Canon must not be merely "interpretable": it should remain the single source of truth and the sole guaranteed point of reversibility. Therefore, the minimum node structure serves a dual function: in the human phase it sustains production and organization of knowledge without contaminating it with system metadata; in the converted phase it guarantees interoperability, validation, auditability, and reversibility without loss of original content.

This dual function is governed by a single guiding principle that runs across the system: **the human edits a semantically sufficient interface —TiddlyWiki—; the converter formalizes the canonical representation**. The human does not write canonical JSON nor produce computationally final nodes: they work inside TiddlyWiki, where they can name, classify, relate, and describe content using the environment's native tools (fields, tags, tiddler body). The converter collects that output, validates it, normalizes it, and projects it into the JSON schema that constitutes the Canon. The fields, vocabularies, and structures described in this report are therefore the system's output contract —the form the node adopts once formalized—, not a JSON template for the human to fill in manually. Whenever this document mentions "the human edits" a field, it should be understood as doing so via the TiddlyWiki interface, not by direct JSON editing.

This distinction has a concrete cognitive justification. It is unreasonable to expect a human in the process of learning to simultaneously manage deterministic UUIDs, numerical metacognitive scales, closed enum vocabularies, and nested provenance objects, all in JSON format. By design, the system assumes the author's cognitive capacity is oriented toward meaning-making, not technical serialization. TiddlyWiki acts as the working surface that translates that output into structured fields; the converter completes the formalization the human reasonably cannot and should not be expected to perform.

### Node JSON Schema
Given the guiding principle —the human edits in TiddlyWiki, the converter formalizes the canonical JSON— the node's general JSON schema should be understood as the output contract that articulates both phases, not as a direct editing template. Its role is not merely to store content, but to explicitly preserve the node's identity, semantic classification, relations with other topic elements, provenance, and metacognitive evaluation. With this structure, the tiddler can function both as a unit of human work during editing and as a computationally interpretable unit once processed by the converter.

That said, the general schema should not be interpreted as a homogeneous block. Some of its fields properly belong to the act of human knowledge construction —and therefore directly map to editable TiddlyWiki fields— while others respond to later serialization, validation, or technical exploitation needs and are the exclusive responsibility of the converter. Consequently, the node schema should be seen as the articulation between an editable semantic layer (governed by the human via TiddlyWiki) and a derived structural layer (produced by the converter), both necessary to sustain the transition from human learning to computational processing.

From this structure, each block of the node serves a specific function within the learning flow and the topic's computational processing.

#### Node identification block: id, title
The identification block establishes the uniqueness and traceability of each tiddler. It must allow both human identification (labeling, search, presentation) and technical resolution by automated processes (indexing, relations, auditing, and export). This block is the minimum basis of the node's individuality and ensures its continuity in the knowledge graph over time.

`id` is the node's structural and immutable identity. Its purpose is technical: it is used to reference the tiddler from other nodes, maintain relational integrity, prevent collisions, and preserve traceability across phases, versions, and exports. The `id` must not contain editorial meaning or encode state or versions; any alteration of the `id` breaks relational continuity and is therefore prohibited.

`title` is the human-readable label of the tiddler. It is intended for presentation and search, and may be refined according to editorial criteria (clarity, concision). Substantive changes to `title` should be recorded in `meta.title_history` to preserve auditability and historical context without affecting the node's technical identity.

In the human phase the block facilitates the production, navigation, and organization of knowledge; in the converted phase it guarantees the node's computational stability. By operational convention —documented in the rules section— the `id` will be generated by the `tiddly-data-converter` as a deterministic UUID v5 (for example, `urn:uuid:<hex>`), using a stable namespace and a canonical input (e.g., canonical title + schema version). The converter will record the provenance of the `id` in `provenance` and may store a `meta.provisional_id` if a temporary identifier existed in the human phase.

**Usage rules**

- The `id` is the exclusive responsibility of the converter (`tiddly-data-converter`). It must not be created or transformed by human users. The converter employs a stable namespace and a deterministic input (e.g., canonical title + schema version) to generate UUID v5; store as `urn:uuid:<hex>`.
- The `id` is immutable once assigned by the converter; any change requires creating a new node and linking it via `relations` (type=`replaces`).
- The `id` must not contain editorial semantics or state (do not encode version, `primary_role`, date, etc.).
- All technical references (relations, indexes, exports) must use the `id`.
- The converter will record the provenance of the `id` in `provenance` (e.g., an entry with `method: "generated"` and `source_ref` pointing to the source used) and may add `meta.provisional_id` if the node carried a temporary identifier.
- `title` is editable; record substantive changes in `meta.title_history` with `changed_by`, `changed_at`, and `reason`.
- Do not use `title` to encode state, version, or dependencies; use `meta.status` and `relations` respectively.
- During the human phase, use `title` and other human-readable keys; the converter will resolve human references (titles, slugs) to `id` when converting.
- Automatic validation (converter): verify UUID v5 pattern and uniqueness in the Canon; reject or reconcile collisions.

**Risks this block prevents**

- Duplicate identifiers or collisions in the graph.
- Loss of traceability between versions and exports.
- Ambiguity between technical identity and the human label.
- Misuse of `title` as a substitute for metadata or relations.

#### Semantic classification block: `primary_role`, `secondary_roles`, `tags`
The semantic classification block places each node within the system along two complementary axes: its epistemological nature (`primary_role` and `secondary_roles`) and its thematic/architectural membership (`tags`). If the identification block answers "what node is this", the classification block answers what kind of knowledge it contains and in which contexts it operates.

The system distinguishes two levels of vocabulary for `primary_role`. The first is the general epistemological vocabulary, applicable to all content nodes. The second is a reduced set of system special roles, reserved for nodes whose function is organizational or infrastructural (for example, `orchestrator`). The two levels are not mixed: a content node uses the general vocabulary; an infrastructure node uses special roles.

Each node must declare exactly one `primary_role` (mandatory) that guides reading and interpretation. Optionally it may declare up to two `secondary_roles` that nuance or complement its nature without replacing the strong signal of the main role. The main role functions as the primary classifier for filtering, analysis, and graphing; the secondary roles add precision without creating interpretive ambiguity.

`process` and `procedure` are distinct categories. A `procedure` describes a prescriptive sequence of steps (example: `🎯 4. Flujo de interacción`). A `process` describes a dynamic of transformation, change, or maturation over time (example: `🌀 Desarrollo y Evolución`). This distinction aligns with the separation the architecture makes between scope and the general sequence of work versus the topic's subsequent evolutionary dynamics.

`tags` operate on two functionally distinct levels. At the human level they are searchable, contextual metadata; at the converter and pipeline level, a subset of tags acts as formal keys for partitioning, filtering, and context activation. This duality does not require all tags to be keys: the distinction is established through families with canonical prefixes. The system's base families are `topic:` (thematic domain), `layer:` (architectural layer: documental, bootstrap, core, normative, thematic, session), `core:` (membership to a core nucleus), `channel:` (communication or activation channel) and `session:` (temporality and specific session). Tags without prefixes remain valid as controlled free classification but are not automatically promoted to pipeline keys.

Tags are closely tied to the tiddlers already present in the template: the emojis (`🎯`, `🌀`, `📚`, `🗂`, `🧪`, `🧭`, `🧰`, `🧾`) and hierarchy prefixes (`#`, `##`, `###`, `####`, `--`, `_`) that appear in the titles of the minimal operational tiddlers constitute the primary source of tag vocabulary. At the human level the origin tag may retain visual richness. At the canonical level the converter produces the normalized form (lowercase, without diacritics, spaces→hyphens, no emojis, no ornamental punctuation); the origin tag is preserved as a reference but the canonical tag is the one that enters pipelines, indexes, and partitions. Explicitly declared tags take precedence over those inferred from the title.

Visual markers `🧱` (stability) and `🌀` (evolution) remain as title conventions, not canonical tags. Operational state is governed by `meta.status`. Tags do **not** participate in the node's UUID v5 calculation: they are circumstantial classification, not stable identity; including them would break traceability with any editorial or normalization change.

Once promoted to a canonical pipeline key a tag is **immutable by default**: renaming it requires a controlled migration (synonym map + reference updates). The converter detects collisions and aliases (e.g., `arquitectura-del-desarrollo`, `arquitectura_desarrollo`, `Arquitectura del desarrollo`) using a synonym map; it proposes automatic resolution and escalates to manual review cases with real semantic ambiguity.

Operational tag limit: a maximum of **20 tags per node** is recommended. Exceeding that threshold produces a converter warning; surpassing 35–40 triggers configurable review/rejection. The underlying problem is not the computational cost of the tag, but the possible semantic degradation of the node: an excess usually indicates the node mixes too many functions or that tags are compensating for a lack of structural clarity.

A session tiddler may share domain, axis, or channel tags with a core nucleus when both work the same scope; it must also include at least one tag from the `session:` family to preserve its situated production identity. Tag propagation between nodes is **opt-in**, defined by an explicit converter rule or editorial policy, never automatic or blind.

#### Relations block: relations
The relations block constitutes the structural mechanism through which tiddlers cease to operate as isolated units and become part of an explicit knowledge network. Its primary function is to record the semantic links between nodes, allowing each unit of information to be situated within a broader relational architecture. Thanks to this block, the system not only classifies or stores content but also makes it possible to reconstruct dependencies, hierarchies, alternatives, incompatibilities, and navigation trajectories between different elements of the subject.

Each relation is composed of two fundamental elements: `type` and `target`. The `type` field defines the nature of the semantic link, while `target` identifies, via canonical UUID v5, the node to which the relation points. In this way, the system can formally represent operations such as using, requiring, being part of, defining, replacing, offering an alternative, or indicating incompatibility. This relational explicitness is essential because it prevents connections between ideas from remaining implicit, scattered, or solely dependent on human interpretation. Instead of relying on vague associations, the system requires declaring how one node is linked to another, thereby strengthening the coherence of the knowledge graph.

Within the learning flow, the relations block fulfills a dual function. In the human editing phase, it forces the author to think of knowledge not only as content but as structure: each idea must be situated in relation to others, which promotes a more systemic understanding of the subject. In the converted phase, these relations allow the system to be computationally processed as an explicit network of dependencies and traversal paths. For this reason, relations not only organizes knowledge: it also enables its navigation, analysis, and automated reuse in contexts such as knowledge graphs, semantic search engines, structural analysis, or artificial intelligence.

Furthermore, this block fulfills a decisive function in knowledge traversal. If `primary_role` and `tags` allow recognizing what type of node is being visited and in which thematic zone it is located, `relations` function as the effective routes of semantic transit between nodes. In an urban analogy, they could be compared to the streets, avenues, and intersections that connect significant places within a city. Thanks to them, a person, an AI, or an analysis system not only knows where it is but also where it can advance, which paths are dependent, which routes are alternatives, and which connections should be avoided. In this way, the relations block transforms the system into a navigable space, where knowledge is not only preserved but can also be traversed in a directed, coherent, and structurally interpretable manner.

##### Block design decisions

The relations block adopts a set of design decisions aimed at keeping the cognitive load low for the human author, limiting the technical complexity for the AI assistant, and preserving the coherence of the canon without introducing hidden automatisms:

- **Simple edge, not rich entity.** Each relation is a lightweight object `{ type, target }` without its own identity (no `id`, no version, no discussion). If a relation needs justification or context, that content belongs to the node's `content`, its `provenance`, or a session hypothesis, not to the edge itself.
- **No automatic inverse relations.** If A `part_of` B, the canon does **not** automatically generate B `contains` A. Inverses can be derived in queries or visualization, but are not persisted as base edges. This avoids duplication, inconsistencies, and graph explosion.
- **No persisted transitivity.** If A `part_of` B and B `part_of` C, the canon does **not** write A `part_of` C. Transitive inferences are resolved at query, analysis, or visualization time, never as newly stored edges.
- **Reference resolution by the converter.** In the human phase, the author works with readable names or titles when declaring relations. The `tiddly-data-converter` resolves each human reference to the corresponding canonical UUID v5. If it cannot resolve a reference, it warns or rejects depending on the pipeline stage. The final canon does **not** contain nonexistent IDs or broken references.
- **Closed and governed vocabulary.** The current seven types (`uses`, `requires`, `part_of`, `defines`, `replaces`, `alternative_to`, `do_not_combine_with`) cover dependency, belonging, definition, substitution, alternatives, and incompatibilities. Any new type must be registered in `🗂🧱 Principios de Gestión` and reviewed before being admitted into the converter.
- **Automatic deduplication.** The converter eliminates duplicate relations (same `type` + same `target`) when formalizing the canon. Having two identical edges provides no value.
- **Conflict detection without automatic resolution.** When the converter detects potentially contradictory relations (e.g., A `replaces` B and B `replaces` A), it flags the conflict and requests human or editorial review. It neither hard-rejects nor automatically resolves.
- **Minimal role-based restrictions.** No heavy matrix is imposed regarding which `primary_role` can relate to which. Only obvious restrictions are applied (e.g., a node cannot `replaces` itself). Finer restrictions may be incorporated in later phases if operational experience justifies them.
- **Versioning tied to the node.** When a node evolves to a new version, the new version declares its own relations. Relations from the previous version remain in the historical node; they are not automatically transferred or archived. If a relation remains valid, it is redeclared or explicitly migrated.

##### Resolution flow: human phase → converted phase

| Step | Responsible              | Action                                                                                           |
| ---- | ------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| 1    | Human (TiddlyWiki)       | Declares relations using readable names or titles as reference to the destination node            |
| 2    | Assisted UI (recommended)| Autocomplete by title, shows resolved ID, warns of duplicates and potential conflicts             |
| 3    | `tiddly-data-converter`  | Resolves each human reference to the canonical UUID v5 of the destination node                   |
| 4    | `tiddly-data-converter`  | Validates existence of the `target`; if unresolved, warns or rejects depending on pipeline stage |
| 5    | `tiddly-data-converter`  | Deduplicates identical edges (`type` + `target`) and flags conflicts for review                  |
| 6    | Canon JSONL              | Stores only valid, resolved, and deduplicated edges: `{ "type": "...", "target": "urn:uuid:…" }` |

**Usage rules**

- Every relation must obligatorily declare `type` and `target`; no additional fields are allowed in the edge.
- `type` must belong to the closed vocabulary of 7 values; ad hoc relations must not be created without review of the glossary and system rules.
- `target` in the canon must be a canonical UUID v5 (`urn:uuid:<hex>`) pointing to an existing node. In the human phase, referencing by readable title is allowed; the converter resolves.
- If the converter cannot resolve a human reference to an existing UUID v5, it must warn or reject depending on the pipeline stage. The canon does **not** contain broken references.
- A relation must express a real link, not a vague or decorative proximity.
- Different relation types must not be mixed in a single implicit declaration.
- `part_of` must be reserved for real structural containment, not for informal grouping.
- `requires` must be used only when real dependency exists, not mere convenience.
- `defines` must not be confused with `uses`: to define is to found or establish; to use is to rely on.
- `replaces` implies functional continuity; if such continuity does not exist, it should not be used.
- A node **cannot** declare `replaces` pointing to itself.
- **Deduplication**: the converter automatically eliminates duplicate relations (same `type` + same `target`) during formalization.
- **No automatic inverses**: the canon does not generate inverse relations. If A `part_of` B, B `contains` A is not persisted. Inverses are derived in queries or visualization.
- **No persisted transitivity**: the canon does not store transitive inferences. If A `part_of` B and B `part_of` C, A `part_of` C is not written. Such chains are resolved at query time.
- **Conflict detection**: when the converter detects potentially contradictory relations (e.g., A `replaces` B and B `replaces` A simultaneously), it flags the conflict and requests human review. It does not automatically resolve.
- **Versioning**: when a node evolves to a new version, the new version declares its own relations. Relations from the previous version remain in the historical node; they are not automatically transferred. If a relation remains valid, it is redeclared or explicitly migrated.
- No rigid restriction matrix of `primary_role` × `relation type` is imposed. Only minimal and obvious restrictions are applied (e.g., self-reference with `replaces`). Finer restrictions may be incorporated in later phases if operational experience justifies them.
- When a new relation seems necessary, it should first be evaluated whether a new type is truly missing or whether the problem stems from a poor application of the existing vocabulary.
- **Governance**: any new relation type must be registered in `🗂🧱 Principios de Gestión` and reviewed before being admitted as a valid value in the converter.

**Risks this block prevents**

- Ambiguity in the links between nodes
- Chaotic graph growth from relations without controlled vocabulary
- Implicit or invisible dependencies
- Confusion between hierarchy, usage, definition, and replacement
- Loss of relational traceability
- Decorative relations without epistemological or structural function
- Duplication of identical edges artificially inflating the graph
- Broken references or nonexistent IDs as `target` in the canon
- Contradictory circular relations (e.g., mutual replacements) going undetected
- Automatic inverses generating inconsistency or silent duplication
- Persisted transitive inferences corrupting the canon's semantics
- Excessive complexity from rich edges (with their own ID, version, or discussion)
- Incorporation of relation types without governance or prior review

#### Content block: content
The content block houses the node's main semantic body: what the node communicates, develops, or preserves. If the identification block distinguishes the node, the classification block situates it, and the relations block connects it, the `content` block is the space where knowledge is expressed in its primary form, edited by the human.

Key decision: the `content` block is now organized into two explicit and complementary subfields, designed to improve display, search, and conversion without forcing the author to duplicate work:

- `content.plain` (mandatory): a brief, flat version of the content intended for summaries, alternative titles, listings, and compact views. It must be plain text (string) and represent an operational summary of the node.
- `content.markdown` (optional): the complete, structured body in Markdown (may include headings, lists, links, KaTeX, code blocks, tables, etc.). It is the usual form of extended editing within TiddlyWiki.

Rationale: separating `plain` and `markdown` allows interfaces and pipelines to consume a lightweight representation without parsing Markdown for every view, while a natural place still exists for the rich body when the author requires it. The converter remains responsible for deriving variants (HTML, normalizations, content-type detectors), but the contract explicitly exposes the presence of both forms.

In the human phase, the author preferably edits `content.markdown` for extensive documents and uses `content.plain` as a summary or main entry in reduced views. In brief sessions the author may fill only `content.plain`; in deeper developments it is recommended to maintain both to facilitate reading and navigation.

In the converted phase, the converter can analyze `content.markdown` to detect content-type (for example, KaTeX equations, tables, code), generate normalized versions (sanitized HTML, LaTeX for equations, MathML), extract derived metadata, and produce output profiles; `content.plain` remains as the canonical summary used in listings, previews, and to generate the canonical `title` in certain pipelines.

From an epistemological standpoint, this organization maintains the separation between human expression (rich text) and the system's operational needs (summary, search, preview), without forcing manual duplication: the converter can suggest a derived `content.plain` when missing, but the author can always edit it.

**Usage rules**

- Every node that intends to communicate knowledge must contain a content block with at least its `content.plain` subfield.
 - `content.plain` is the indispensable subfield for views and searches; `content.markdown` houses the extended development. Any derived variant (presentation, normalization, type detection) is the converter's responsibility.

- The author is free to organize `content.markdown` according to the node's nature: brief formulation, extensive development, technical notation, or coherent combinations. `content.plain` must contain an operational summary of the node when applicable.
- The `content` block must not absorb information that belongs to other blocks, such as provenance, epistemic state, or relations.
- The final technical representation of the content (content-type detection, normalization, presentation variants, etc.) is the converter's responsibility, not the human-editable node's.

**Mathematical content and `equation` role**

When a node has `primary_role: equation` (or `equation` as a secondary role), `content.markdown` houses the mathematical expression in TiddlyWiki's native textual notation—typically KaTeX within Markdown. The author writes the equation as part of the Markdown body. The converter, during the formalization phase, detects the mathematical content-type, validates the syntax, extracts derived metadata (variables, operators, symbolic dependencies), and produces alternative representations (normalized LaTeX, MathML, rendered image) without altering `content.markdown`. In this way, the equation is preserved in its human-editable source form, while its computational exploitation remains in the derived layer.

If the equation requires interpretive context—explanation of variables, domain of application, step-by-step derivation—that context must reside in a separate tiddler linked via `relations` (type = `defines` or `uses`), following the general principle of separation between specialized content and explanation layer described in the corresponding section.

**Risks this block prevents**

- Empty or semantically mute nodes
- Confusion between content and metadata
- Loss of human readability
- Excessive reduction of knowledge to only relations or tags
- Ambiguity about which subfield constitutes the content's source of truth
- Improper mixing of human editing and computational formalization

#### Metadata block: meta
The metadata block fulfills the function of contextualizing the node structurally and epistemologically without altering its original content. Its purpose is to record information that does not belong to the tiddler's expressive core but is indispensable for situating it within the system, evaluating its state, and guiding its subsequent treatment. While the content block preserves what the node says, the meta block specifies the condition in which that knowledge exists, under which schema version it is interpreted, and for what types of use it was intended.

In the node's general schema, this block includes fields belonging to two distinct layers. The `status` and `epistemic_state` fields are governed by the human through TiddlyWiki: the first records the degree of editorial or structural maturation of the node (`draft`, `validated`, `published`, `canonical`), and the second makes explicit the nature of the contained knowledge (`fact`, `hypothesis`, `interpretation`, `intuition`, `definition`, `procedure`, `evidence`). Both fields reflect epistemological decisions that only the author can make. In contrast, `schema_version`, `intended_use`, `title_history`, `status_history`, and `provisional_id` are fields injected or managed by the converter: `schema_version` allows interpreting the node under an explicit structural version (essential for validations and migrations); `intended_use` guides the tiddler's intended use in computational exploitation contexts (BI, AI, statistical, Markov, general); `title_history` preserves the record of substantive title changes with authorship, date, and reason data; `status_history` records each `status` transition with actor, date, and motive, guaranteeing editorial promotion traceability; and `provisional_id` stores the temporary identifier the node may have had before receiving its definitive UUID v5. The human does not need to populate or maintain these five fields: the converter generates, updates, or infers them from the human production.

Within the learning flow, the meta block fulfills an enormously important function because it allows the system to not treat all knowledge as if it had the same level of stability, validation, or use. In the human editing phase, the author-governed fields—`status` and `epistemic_state`—help recognize whether a preliminary intuition, a developing hypothesis, an already consolidated procedure, or verifiable evidence is being recorded. This distinction reduces confusion and prevents content under formation from being prematurely interpreted as stabilized knowledge. In the converted phase, the meta block becomes even more relevant, as the converter injects derived fields (`schema_version`, `intended_use`, `title_history`, `status_history`, `provisional_id`) and facilitates validation, filtering, automatic classification, and node selection processes according to their function or degree of consolidation.

In this way, the metadata block introduces a layer of interpretive discipline within the system. It does not add new content, but it does organize how content should be read, evaluated, and reused. Thanks to this, the node exists not only as a semantic unit but also as a contextualized unit within an architecture that distinguishes between states, purposes, and levels of formalization. In this sense, meta acts as the bridge between the node's content and the conditions under which that content can be understood, validated, and leveraged by both people and computational systems.

##### Block design decisions

The metadata block adopts a set of design decisions aimed at keeping the context layer sober and governable, without turning it into a dense editorial administration surface:

- **Only two human fields.** The human governs exclusively `status` and `epistemic_state` through TiddlyWiki. No free or customized fields are opened within `meta`. If a real need arises later, it is evaluated through governance, but not anticipated.
- **Closed vocabularies for both human fields.** Both `status` and `epistemic_state` use strict enums. Custom states and free epistemological labels are not allowed. The operational value of these fields lies in their being short, stable, and comprehensible by human and AI alike.
- **Five converter fields.** `schema_version`, `intended_use`, `title_history`, `status_history`, and `provisional_id` are the exclusive responsibility of the `tiddly-data-converter`. The human does not create, edit, or maintain them.
- **Auditing for critical fields.** `title_history` and `status_history` record each transition with actor (`changed_by`), date (`changed_at`), and reason (`reason`). This is not equivalent to an approval workflow, but rather minimal traceability: who changed what, when, and why.
- **`meta` excluded from UUID v5.** No field from `meta` participates in the identifier calculation. The UUID must remain stable even if state, editorial classification, title history, intended use, or other derived layers change.
- **Authorship and origin outside `meta`.** `created_by` and `created_at` do not belong to this block; they reside in `provenance`. Meta covers state and operational purpose; provenance covers origin and authorship.
- **No pipeline technical fields.** Hashes, conversion timestamps, detected content-type, and other pipeline technical data are not stored in `meta`; they correspond to internal converter layers.

- Free or customized fields turning `meta` into a catch-all drawer
- Duplication of authorship/origin between `meta` and `provenance`
- UUID v5 instability from including editable metadata in its calculation

#### Provenance block: provenance
The provenance block fulfills the function of recording the origin and the mode of obtaining the knowledge contained in the node. Its purpose is to make explicit where the information comes from, under what modality it was produced, with what tools or actors it was co-constructed, and, when applicable, what reference allows tracing its source. In this way, the system does not merely preserve content but also preserves the minimum conditions for evaluating its reliability, its production context, and its possible critical reuse.

In structural terms, `provenance` is modeled as a **list of entries**, not as a single object. This responds to the reality of the system's workflow: most tiddlers are not purely human, nor purely AI-generated, nor purely external. The typical production cycle is conversational and progressive: the human reads, studies, or researches a topic; opens a conversation with an AI assistant; shares the template and the technical report; the AI helps concretize tiddler by tiddler; the human edits the TiddlyWiki template; and, once the converter is available, the document is canonized into JSONL. From there, tools like VS Code with Copilot can continue adding lines to the Canon, always under the declared rules, with the guarantee that the converter's reversibility allows reopening the enriched HTML. This flow produces a **mixed, progressive, and conversational provenance** that a single rigid label (`human` / `ai` / `external`) cannot adequately reflect. The entry list allows recording each contribution layer without losing the node's genealogy.

Each provenance entry is composed of mandatory and optional fields. The mandatory fields are `actor` (who contributed), `origin` (source type), `method` (obtainment modality), and `source_ref` (traceable reference). The optional fields enrich traceability without adding load on the human: `timestamp` (completed by the converter), `model` (name and version of the AI model when `origin = ai`), `session_ref` (link or id of the conversation), `snippet` (brief fragment that recalls the type of intervention), `verified_by` and `verified_at` (signal that a human reviewed an AI contribution). Together, these components make rich traceability of knowledge origin possible without forcing the human to manually fill technical fields.

Within the learning flow, the `provenance` block fulfills a central epistemological function, as it prevents all assertions from being treated as if they shared the same provenance or the same level of legitimacy. In the human editing phase, this block helps the author recognize whether an idea comes from direct observation, reading, personal synthesis, artificial assistance, or a combination of sources. The human can declare the initial provenance, correct the origin, add sources, or validate human-AI mixes. In the converted phase, the converter completes timestamps, normalizes formats, injects `session_ref` and `model`, and generates the canonical form of each entry. Provenance then becomes a powerful criterion for auditing, filtering, validation, and computational analysis, as it allows tracing which part of the system depends on human production, which part comes from external sources, which part has been generated or mediated by artificial intelligence tools, and with which specific model.

The importance of this block in modern times is especially high, given that one of the central challenges of contemporary knowledge systems consists of not losing the link between information and its origin. In contexts marked by documentary abundance, increasing automation, and collaboration with generative models, provenance ceases to be a secondary detail and becomes a condition of intelligibility and trust. For this reason, `provenance` fulfills not only an administrative function but also a critical one: it allows each node to preserve memory of its production genealogy and enables the system to evaluate, with greater precision, what type of authority, dependency, or mediation accompanies each unit of knowledge.

This design rationale draws directly on insights from recent provenance management literature. Kleinsteuber et al. argue that "proper provenance management" is essential for understanding data origins, increasing trust, and enabling "reproducibility and updatability" in knowledge graph platforms [3]. Samuel et al.'s ProvBook extends this concern to human-facing authoring environments, demonstrating that provenance co-located with editable artifacts —rather than stored in a separate log— supports "trust and reproducibility" while preserving round-trip convertibility between human and machine representations [4]. And Skjæveland et al. observe that "provenance data is also vital" for personal knowledge systems and that metadata will typically exceed the primary data in size [5] —a prediction this system's canonical nodes empirically validate. A detailed structural mapping against each of these frameworks appears in the architectural alignment sections above.

##### Block design decisions

The provenance block adopts a set of design decisions aimed at reflecting the real production flow (mixed, conversational, progressive) without adding unnecessary load on the human:

- **Provenance as a list, not a single object.** A tiddler can have multiple `provenance` entries (human reading + conversation with AI + final integration + subsequent editing). Each entry records a contribution layer with its own actor, method, and reference.
- **Mandatory AI model registration.** When `origin = ai`, the `model` field must record the model's name and version or variant (e.g., `gpt-5-mini`, `claude-opus-4.6`). This should not be a burden on the human; ideally the converter or assisted layer completes it.
- **Link to session, not just a flag.** When AI participated in the node's production, `session_ref` stores an id or link to the conversation (chat-id, URL, internal id). This allows tracing from which conversation the tiddler emerged, with which model, and in what context.
- **Brief snippet, never complete prompt.** An optional `snippet` is allowed that recalls the type of intervention. Complete prompts and complete responses are not stored within `provenance`: that would inflate the node, duplicate information, and could compromise privacy. The complete transcript lives outside the Canon, referenced by `session_ref`.
- **Registrable human verification.** When a node has relevant AI provenance, `verified_by` and `verified_at` allow leaving a signal that a human reviewed what was integrated. It is not a heavy approval workflow but a minimal validation signal.
- **Both actors can create and edit.** The human can declare initial provenance, correct origin, add sources, and validate human-AI mixes. The converter can complete timestamps, normalize formats, inject `session_ref`, register `model`, and generate the canonical form. This is consistent with the system's guiding principle.
- **`source_ref` accepts combined formats.** URL, DOI, `internal_id`, controlled file name/path. It is not restricted to a single format because the system works with chats, PDFs, articles, internal nodes, and local resources.
- **Complete history in the Canon.** The entire entry list is preserved without truncation. In derived views or public exports, a summarized version may be shown.
- **Redaction in public exports.** When the Canon is exported outside the working environment, `session_ref`, sensitive snippets, and unnecessary internal details are redacted or summarized. The general origin and AI participation are preserved.

**Usage rules**

- `provenance` is a list of entries; each entry records a contribution layer to the node.
- The `provenance` block does not describe the content or its final validity; it describes its origin, its production genealogy, and its mode of obtainment.
- Each entry must include at least `actor`, `origin`, `method`, and `source_ref`. The remaining fields are optional but recommended when applicable.
- `origin` and `method` use closed vocabularies (enums); ad hoc values are not allowed.
- When `origin = ai`, the `model` field is mandatory and must record the model's name and version or variant (e.g., `gpt-5-mini`, `claude-opus-4.6`).
- `session_ref` must record an id or link to the conversation when the contribution emerged from a chat. The complete transcript is not stored within the Canon.
- `snippet` is optional and brief. Complete prompts and complete responses are not stored within `provenance`.
- `verified_by` and `verified_at` are used when a human reviews and validates a contribution with `origin = ai`. It is not a heavy approval workflow but a minimal validation signal.
- `source_ref` accepts combined formats: URL, DOI, `internal_id`, controlled file name/path.
- **Both** actors (human and converter) can create and edit `provenance`. The human declares initial provenance, corrects origin, adds sources, and validates. The converter completes timestamps, normalizes, injects derived fields, and generates the canonical form.
- `provenance` must not be confused with `meta.epistemic_state`: one addresses origin and production genealogy, the other the nature of knowledge.
- The fact that content has `origin = ai` does not invalidate the node, but it does require clarity to avoid Canon contamination.
- The complete entry history is preserved in the Canon without truncation. In derived views or public exports, a summarized version may be shown.
- **Public exports**: when the Canon is exported outside the working environment, `session_ref`, sensitive snippets, and unnecessary internal details are redacted or summarized. The general origin and AI participation are preserved.
- A node may change `status` or `epistemic_state` over time, but its provenance must be preserved intact as part of its history.

**Risks this block prevents**

- Confusion between own, external, or generated content
- Loss of an assertion's origin
- Uncritical use of AI-produced content
- Mixing direct observation and inference
- Difficulty auditing or reviewing sources
- Canon contamination with insufficiently traceable content
- Inability to trace which AI model contributed to a node
- Loss of the link to the conversation from which the tiddler emerged
- Unverified AI contributions circulating as authoritative sources
- Complete prompts or responses inflating the node and compromising privacy
- Rigid provenance (single object) unable to reflect the actual mixed flow
- Exposure of sensitive internal details in public exports

#### Metacognition block: metacognition
The metacognition block fulfills the function of recording the reflective position of the author or the human system regarding the node's content. Its purpose is not to describe what the node says, where it comes from, or how it is classified, but to make explicit the degree of certainty, openness, or uncertainty with which that content is being held at the moment of its formulation. In this way, `metacognition` introduces a minimal layer of epistemological awareness that allows distinguishing between clearly affirmed content, provisional content, ambiguous content, or content that requires further elaboration.

In the human phase, this block is especially important because it helps avoid one of the most frequent drifts in knowledge systems: treating every assertion as if it had the same level of clarity, maturity, or confidence. Thanks to `metacognition`, the node can preserve not only its content but also the cognitive position with which it was written. This allows the system to record not only knowledge but also the reflective state from which that knowledge was produced, read, or used. Within this flow, AI can help significantly: asking validation questions, helping to ground the level of understanding, and proposing values. However, the human always retains the final word on the values of `certainty`, `ambiguity`, and `notes`.

In the converted phase, `metacognition` offers valuable information for subsequent analyses, confidence filters, node selection, and traceability of the learning process. A node with high certainty and low ambiguity does not occupy the same operational place as one with low certainty and high ambiguity, even though both are valid within the system. Furthermore, these signals allow the system to be more honest in the face of uncertainty and ensure that derived processes—including AI or computational analysis—do not interpret all nodes as equally firm or consolidated.

From an epistemological standpoint, this block is important because it recognizes that knowledge is not produced solely as objective content but also as a situated relationship between the subject and what they affirm. In this sense, `metacognition` is not an introspective ornament but an explicit mechanism for recording the degree of confidence, openness, and reservation with which knowledge is being held. Thus, the system can better detect its own limits and reduce the risk of turning weak intuitions into implicit certainties.

In the node schema, this block is composed of three human-editable fields (`certainty`, `ambiguity`, `notes`) and one converter-managed field (`metacognition_history`). The `certainty` field indicates the provisional confidence level the author attributes to the content, while `ambiguity` records the degree of openness, indeterminacy, or interpretive difficulty that still accompanies the node. Both fields, expressed on a discrete `0–3` scale, do not seek to offer an absolute measure but rather an operational reference that helps situate the cognitive state of the content within the learning process. The `notes` field, for its part, offers a space to make explicit reflective observations, doubts, warnings, nuances, or criteria that do not fit directly into the main content but are relevant for understanding how the node should be read or reviewed. The `metacognition_history` field preserves the change history by version, recording actor and timestamp of each modification without adding load on the human.

Within the learning flow, the `metacognition` block plays a decisive function because it allows the system to incorporate awareness of its own degrees of maturation. In the human editing phase, this block helps the author distinguish between what they consider relatively clear and what still remains open, tentative, or insufficiently articulated. This reduces the risk of presenting as consolidated what is still under exploration, and fosters a more honest and precise relationship with one's own knowledge process. In the converted phase, these indicators can be used for auditing, filtering, review prioritization, or analysis of the system's general state, making visible not only what knowledge exists but also how much confidence or ambiguity accompanies each node.

In this sense, metacognition introduces a particularly valuable dimension in a system oriented toward both human learning and computational processing. While other blocks identify, classify, relate, or contextualize content, `metacognition` records how that content is evaluated from within the knowledge construction process itself. Thanks to this, the system can recognize that not every node is at the same level of clarity or consolidation, and that uncertainty does not necessarily constitute a flaw but rather a legitimate and documentable part of learning. Thus, the metacognition block contributes to making the system's architecture not only structurally rigorous but also epistemologically reflective.

##### Block design decisions

The metacognition block adopts a set of design decisions aimed at keeping it as an operational signal of clarity and openness, without transforming it into a heavy psychological layer or introspective bureaucracy:

- **Three human fields, 0–3 scale.** The human edits `certainty`, `ambiguity`, and `notes`. The discrete `0–3` scale provides sufficient resolution without simulating false precision. No `0–5` scales, percentages, or decimals are adopted.
- **Default values when creating a tiddler.** `certainty: 1` (initial understanding) and `ambiguity: 2` (several things still open). This represents the typical startup state: there is a minimum basis to work with, but openness remains. `0/0` is not used because it rarely corresponds to the actual beginning of a topic.
- **`notes` mandatory when content is fragile.** `notes` is recommended/required when `certainty ≤ 1` or `ambiguity ≥ 2`. This prevents an isolated number from lacking context and helps both AI and human correctly interpret the node's state.
- **No automatic `confidence_score` in the base contract.** No normalized `0.0–1.0` score is added as an additional field. `certainty` and `ambiguity` already exist; duplicating semantics adds unnecessary complexity. If a pipeline needs a derived score, it can calculate it as a derived layer outside the Canon.
- **Minimal auditing: actor + timestamp.** Each `metacognition` change is recorded in `metacognition_history` with `changed_by` and `changed_at`. `reason` is not required by default (only for important changes) to avoid unnecessary friction.
- **History by version, not blind overwriting.** The node shows the current state of `metacognition`; the converter preserves the change history by version or snapshot in `metacognition_history`. This maintains lightness for human editing with sufficient traceability for auditing.
- **Public exports: summarized notes.** In the working environment, `notes` is shown in full. In public exports, they are summarized or redacted if they contain overly internal reflections (raw doubts, intimate learning observations).
- **AI assists, human confirms.** AI can suggest values, propose `notes` formulations, and help ground the cognitive state through questions. The converter records changes and normalizes format. But the human retains the final word on the three editable fields.
- **Link to provenance when the update comes from AI.** When a `metacognition` adjustment arises from an assisted conversation, the system can link `session_ref`, `model`, and eventually `verified_by` from the corresponding `provenance` entry. This reinforces the system's honesty: it was not an isolated self-assessment but an evaluation aided by human-AI interaction.

**Usage rules**

- The `metacognition` block does not substitute content or the node's epistemic state; it records the reflective position regarding them.
- `certainty`, `ambiguity`, and `notes` are the fields the human directly governs. AI can suggest values and propose formulations, but the human confirms.
- `certainty` and `ambiguity` use a discrete `0–3` scale (strict enum); no other ranges or decimal values are allowed.
- **Default values** when creating a tiddler: `certainty: 1`, `ambiguity: 2`. They represent initial understanding with typical startup openness.
- `notes` is optional by default, but is recommended/required when `certainty ≤ 1` or `ambiguity ≥ 2`, so that the number does not lack context.
- A high `certainty` value does not automatically convert the content into a fact; it only indicates greater cognitive confidence.

- **Auditing**: each `metacognition` change is recorded in `metacognition_history` with `changed_by` and `changed_at`. The `reason` field is optional and only recommended for important changes.

- **No `confidence_score` in the base contract.** No additional normalized score is added. A pipeline that needs one can derive it as an external layer.
- Metacognition can change over time without altering the node's structural identity, so it should be understood as a situated and revisable signal.
- **Link to provenance**: when a `metacognition` adjustment arises from AI interaction, the system can link `session_ref`, `model`, and `verified_by` from the corresponding `provenance` entry.
- The converter records changes, normalizes format, and preserves history. The human does not need to maintain `metacognition_history` manually.

- Treating all knowledge as equally certain
- Hiding ambiguity behind affirmative writing
- Confusing intuitions with mature nodes
- Losing the cognitive context from which the content was written
- Forcing false objectivity on still-open content
- Hindering critical review of the system itself
- `certainty`/`ambiguity` values without context when content is fragile

### Valid data types within blocks

Once the general node structure has been defined and the function of each block described, it becomes necessary to specify the valid data types that the schema admits for each field. This specification does not belong strictly to the normative level of the rules, but rather to the syntactic level of the model, as it establishes the technical form that each node component must adopt in order to be interpreted, validated, and processed consistently.

The importance of this precision lies in the fact that the system does not only organize knowledge from a conceptual perspective, but must also guarantee compatibility with validators, converters, derived profiles, and subsequent computational exploitation processes. Consequently, each block of the node requires a defined data form—not to arbitrarily restrict human editing, but to ensure that the recorded information retains a sufficiently stable and computationally interpretable structure.

In general terms, the valid data types of the schema are as follows:

id: string
title: string
primary_role: enum
secondary_roles: array
tags: array
relations: array
content.plain: string
content.markdown: string
meta: object
provenance: array
metacognition: object

This specification establishes the minimum syntactic base of the system and ensures that each node retains not only semantic coherence, but also structural validity within the editing, conversion, and subsequent reuse process

### Human data interface and ingestion for the node (editable schema)
The human data interface and ingestion for the node corresponds to the set of fields that the author can create, edit, and refine directly during the study process within TiddlyWiki. In accordance with the system's governing principle—**the human edits a sufficient semantic interface (TiddlyWiki); the converter formalizes the canonical representation**—this interface does not aim to reproduce the complete JSON schema, but rather to offer a cognitive working surface where the author can name, classify, relate, and describe content in formation using TiddlyWiki's native tools (title, fields, tags, tiddler body), while maintaining a structural base that makes subsequent formalization by the converter possible.

Within this interface, the human governs the fields belonging to the semantic core of knowledge: title, primary_role, secondary_roles, tags, relations, content.plain, content.markdown, meta.status, meta.epistemic_state, provenance, and metacognition. The following fields are **not** part of human editing and are the exclusive responsibility of the converter: `id` (deterministic UUID v5), `meta.schema_version`, `meta.intended_use`, `meta.title_history`, `meta.status_history`, `meta.provisional_id`, and `metacognition.metacognition_history`. These fields make it possible to record not only the node's content, but also its epistemological intention, its thematic placement, its initial connections, its provenance, and the degree of certainty or ambiguity that accompanies what is being written. The requirement at this stage should be understood as structurally sufficient but not rigidly maximalist: the node must possess a minimum recognizable form, but it is not required that all its information be completely stabilized from the very first moment.

It is important to recognize that the human capacity to populate all these fields with precision is limited, especially during the exploratory phases of learning. The author may not yet be clear whether their content is a hypothesis or an intuition, may not have the exact provenance reference, or may not be able to reliably calibrate the levels of `certainty` and `ambiguity`. This limitation is not a user defect, but a cognitive reality that the system assumes by design. For this reason, the human-editable schema allows certain aspects of the node to remain incomplete or provisional, especially when the knowledge is in early stages of development. It is valid, for example, for a tiddler in draft state to have still-partial relations, tags still under review, or approximate metacognitive notes. Similarly, some fields may function as optional in early phases, provided that this openness does not destroy the minimum intelligibility of the node. This flexibility does not represent a weakness of the system, but a necessary condition for learning to develop without being subjected from the outset to excessive formal bureaucracy. Thus, the human interface of the node balances two complementary needs: on one hand, offering a structure sufficient to sustain the organization of knowledge; on the other, preserving a margin of openness that allows exploring, reformulating, and maturing ideas before their computational consolidation.

### Derived computational formalization (converter)
The computational formalization of the node corresponds to the set of technical operations that the converter applies to the tiddler once its human editing phase has been finalized, or sufficiently stabilized. Its purpose is not to replace the node or reinterpret its content, but to formalize it, validate it, and project it within a reversible computational infrastructure. At this level, the tiddler ceases to operate solely as a cognitive work unit and becomes integrated into an architecture where it can be serialized, audited, indexed, reverted, and derived without losing its original content. The converter therefore acts as a mediator between the human construction of knowledge and its subsequent technical circulation, preserving the node as a semantic source and transforming it into an object suitable for Canon JSONL, structured reverse, and analytical profiles.

The elements belonging to this layer are not part of the editable semantic core of the tiddler, but rather of the technical infrastructure that guarantees its operational consistency. Among them are schema_version, timestamps, hashes, technical content-type detection, normalizations, output manifests, errors treated as auditable datasets, regenerable indexes, and derived profiles. Each one fulfills a precise function within the formalization process. schema_version allows interpreting the node under an explicit structural version; timestamps record relevant technical events; hashes ensure integrity, comparison, and change control; content-type identifies the technical nature of the content without depending on human editing; manifests describe what was generated, under which profile, and with what scope; errors, when recorded as a dataset, cease to be operational noise and become auditable evidence of the system's state; regenerable indexes enable navigation, search, and efficient access without becoming a source of truth; and derived profiles prepare knowledge for specific uses without altering the Canon.

The importance of this layer lies in that it materializes the second half of the governing principle: the converter formalizes the canonical representation. This implies maintaining a rigorous separation between what the human produces through TiddlyWiki and what the machine needs to operate reliably. The converter does not add meaning to the content, but it does add technical structure, validation, observability, and reversibility. From this layer, the Canon JSONL can be produced as the single source of truth; secondly, reverse outputs (JSON and, subsequently, HTML); and only then the corresponding analytical derivatives for BI, AI, statistical-qualitative analysis, or Markov-type governance. Consequently, computational formalization should not be understood as an arbitrary expansion of the node, but as the derived layer that makes possible its technical circulation, auditability, and reconstruction, without compromising the semantic integrity of the original knowledge.

### Classification of system rules
Once the difference between the human interface of the node and its subsequent computational formalization has been established, it becomes possible to introduce the classification of the rules governing the system. This classification does not respond solely to a need for expository order, but to an architectural requirement of the model itself: not all rules fulfill the same function, nor should they all be applied with the same degree of rigidity at every moment of the flow. Some protect the minimum structural integrity of the system, others guide good practices in semantic construction, and others must preserve sufficient flexibility so as not to block the intellectual exploration that characterizes the human phase of knowledge.

For this reason, the system's rules are organized into three normative levels: obligatory, recommended, and flexible. Obligatory rules guarantee the technical validity and minimum coherence of the node; recommended rules promote clarity, consistency, and semantic quality; and flexible rules preserve the possibility of recording knowledge in formation without imposing premature formalism. This distinction allows balancing structural discipline and intellectual exploration within a single architecture, making it possible for the system to be simultaneously computationally reliable and cognitively fertile.

#### Obligatory Rules
Obligatory rules constitute the minimum normative core of the system. Their function is to ensure that each node retains the indispensable structural conditions to be identified, interpreted, related, and processed without technical ambiguities incompatible with the general architecture. Unlike recommended or flexible rules, these do not operate as suggestions or contextual criteria, but as validity conditions whose absence compromises the integrity of the node or the reliability of the system as a whole.

In this sense, obligatory rules should not be understood as arbitrary restrictions imposed on learning, but as the foundation that allows the knowledge produced to retain form, continuity, traceability, and interoperability. Thanks to them, the system can admit intellectual exploration without collapsing into structural disorder, and can sustain conversion, analysis, audit, and reuse processes without losing internal consistency. Their role, therefore, consists of protecting that which cannot be left to interpretive variation without jeopardizing the stability of the model.

##### Unique node identity
Unique node identity constitutes a fundamental structural condition of the system. Its purpose is to guarantee that each tiddler can be recognized as an unrepeatable entity within the general architecture, maintaining continuity throughout its relations, transformations, and exports. Without this condition, the system would lose the ability to sustain a stable knowledge network, as it could not precisely distinguish between different nodes nor preserve the traceability of their links over time.

This rule is materialized in the id field, which must function as a unique, persistent, and immutable identifier of the node. Its value must not be repeated within the system, nor modified once the tiddler has been created, as any alteration would break its structural continuity and affect the relations that depend on it. In operational terms, this identity can be expressed through a deterministic identifier, such as UUID v5, or through another equivalent convention, provided it guarantees stability, uniqueness, and consistency between human, converted, and exported phases.

Within the system's flow, unique identity does not fulfill a merely editorial function, but a relational and computational one. In the human editing phase, it allows distinguishing the node from other elements of the topic; in the converted phase, it ensures that the tiddler can be referenced, validated, related, and indexed without ambiguity. Thanks to this rule, the system can build reliable knowledge graphs, sustain consistent data pipelines, and preserve traceability between versions, exports, and formalization processes. For this reason, unique node identity is not an optional convention, but an obligatory condition for the structural existence of the tiddler within the system.

##### Minimum node structure with obligatory rules
The minimum node structure constitutes the basic internal integrity condition that every tiddler must satisfy in order to be part of the system. Its function is to ensure that each unit of knowledge possesses, at a minimum, the indispensable components to be identified, interpreted, contextualized, and processed without losing its essential coherence. In this sense, the minimum structure does not require that the knowledge be completely mature or semantically stabilized from the outset, but that the node retains a recognizable form that allows distinguishing its fundamental dimensions.

In the human editing phase, this minimum structure corresponds to the irreducible set of blocks through which the author can record a unit of knowledge in a sufficiently organized manner. These blocks allow preserving the node's identity, its semantic classification, its initial relations, its content, its epistemological state, its provenance, and its metacognitive reflection. Thanks to this, even a tiddler in an early state can exist as part of the system without being reduced to a scattered or informal annotation.

The minimum requirement, therefore, does not imply that all fields must be fully developed from the start. It is admissible, for example, for a draft-state node to have empty tags, not-yet-defined relations, or provisional metacognitive notes. What this rule requires is that the tiddler retains a basic architecture sufficient to be recognized, interpreted, and subsequently formalized by the converter. In this way, the minimum node structure functions as the elementary support that allows exploratory learning to be transformed, without rupture, into traceable and computationally reusable knowledge.

## Specialized content treatment and explanation layer
Uren et al. provide a foundational theoretical basis for this treatment, arguing that ontology-based semantic annotation can transform ordinary documents into "intelligent" resources that support "enhanced information retrieval" and interoperability [6]. Their seven-requirement framework for semantic annotation systems in knowledge management —particularly the requirements for annotation storage, document-evolution consistency, and vocabulary support— maps with substantial precision onto the architectural decisions developed in this section. A detailed alignment appears in the corresponding section above.

Within the system's architecture, it is recognized that not all knowledge adopts a uniform form nor responds to the same conditions of reading, interpretation, or reuse. There exist tiddlers whose content can be directly comprehensible to a person, but there also exist others whose principal value resides in their technical or formal specialization. This specialization does not arise from an arbitrary external classification, but from the content types themselves that TiddlyWiki can host natively or compatibly, including plain text, HTML, JSON data, text/csv, markdown blocks, KaTeX as textual notation, code, styles, images, and other formats oriented toward preservation, analysis, or development. Within this framework, the system must not force an artificial homogenization of content, but rather preserve each tiddler in the form that best expresses its original nature.

Under this logic, the treatment of specialized content starts from a simple principle: the content must be kept intact in its source form, while its interpretation, contextualization, or explanation can be organized in a separate layer. This allows the system to preserve the integrity of knowledge without sacrificing its intelligibility. A tiddler containing tabular data in text/csv, a JSON structure, a markdown block, an equation in textual KaTeX notation, a data dictionary, or a technical fragment does not need to become self-explanatory within the same node to be valid; it suffices that it be correctly preserved and that the system offers structured mechanisms to relate it to other nodes that provide human, conceptual, or methodological context.

In the case of data, this architecture proves especially important. When a dataset can be incorporated into the system in a compatible format, it ceases to be an external file without epistemological status and becomes a legitimate node within the knowledge network. This means that the data no longer functions solely as a peripheral input, but as a preservable, relatable, traceable, and reusable unit in subsequent processes of statistical analysis, artificial intelligence, modeling, or reversible reconstruction. The same applies to mathematical expressions, JSON structures, or markdown blocks: they are not merely auxiliary supports, but contents that can inhabit the system as constitutive parts of knowledge.

The existence of an explanation layer fulfills an essential function. On one hand, it prevents specialized content from being altered, simplified, or destructively “translated” in the name of readability. On the other hand, it allows knowledge to remain accessible for reading, learning, human navigation, and subsequent AI use. Instead of mixing the data with its explanation, the equation with its interpretation, or the technical block with its commentary, the system proposes an explicit separation between both levels: specialized content remains as source, while the explanation is modeled as another tiddler, structurally linked when necessary.

This approach responds to both a technical and epistemological necessity. Technically, it allows sustaining reversibility, traceability, and consistency without forcing the converter to rewrite that which must be preserved. Epistemologically, it recognizes that understanding does not always mean reformulating the original content, but rather situating it within a network of relations that grants it meaning. Consequently, the system does not demand that every node be self-sufficient, but it does guarantee that every node can be integrated into a comprehensible, navigable, and usable structure by both humans and machines.

Consequently, the treatment of specialized content and the existence of an explanation layer do not constitute secondary elements, but rather a necessary condition for sustaining a knowledge system that aims to be, simultaneously, faithful to the origin, useful for computational analysis, and comprehensible to human beings. The resulting architecture does not eliminate the complexity of knowledge, but rather organizes it in differentiated levels, allowing each type of content to preserve its function without losing connection with the rest of the system.

### Specialized content within the system
The system recognizes that tiddlers do not constitute a homogeneous content unit, but rather a structure capable of hosting different types of information according to the nature of the knowledge to be preserved. In this regard, one of the most important starting points is that TiddlyWiki already has a native set of content types that allow representing text, images, data structures, and development-oriented elements. This implies that the system's architecture does not need to impose an artificial typology from scratch, but rather build upon an already existing base to organize, preserve, and relate contents of different natures.

Among the content types that TiddlyWiki natively supports are textual formats such as HTML markup, Markdown, Plain text, and TiddlyWiki 5; image formats such as GIF, JPEG, PNG, and SVG; and formats oriented toward development or information structuring, such as Compound tiddler, Data dictionary, JavaScript code, JSON data, and Static stylesheet. This diversity allows the system to naturally incorporate contents very different from each other, without forcing them to reduce to a single expressive form or a single logic of representation. Consequently, a tiddler can contain an explanation in natural language, a set of tabular data in a compatible format, an equation written in textual notation, a JSON block, or a more complex technical structure, and still remain a valid unit within the system.

From this base, specialized content is understood as that whose principal function is not necessarily immediate reading by a person, but rather the preservation of a technical, structured, or semantically dense form of knowledge. This includes, for example, tabular data incorporated through compatible formats, mathematical expressions, JSON content, data dictionaries, code fragments, or any other representation whose value depends more on its formal exactness than on its initial readability. In all these cases, the system starts from the principle that content must be maintained in the form that best respects its original nature.

This decision has an important consequence: the system does not require that all specialized content be self-explanatory nor that every unit of knowledge adopt a format immediately readable by humans. On the contrary, it admits that certain tiddlers may fulfill a primarily technical function, while their explanation, interpretation, or contextualization may reside in other nodes of the system. Thus, the diversity of TiddlyWiki's native content types is not considered a problem to correct, but rather a structural capability that allows representing complex knowledge in the most adequate form, as long as that form remains integrated within a traceable, reversible, and comprehensible architecture.

Within this framework, content specialization does not imply isolation. Although content types may differ, all tiddlers share the same vocation within the system: being able to be preserved, related, contextualized, and eventually reconstructed without losing continuity with their origin. In this way, specialized content ceases to be a difficult-to-manage exception and becomes a legitimate part of the general knowledge architecture.

### Separation between content and explanation
Within the system's architecture, a fundamental distinction is established between specialized content and the explanation that allows its interpretation. This separation responds to the need to preserve the integrity of knowledge without forcing it to adopt an artificially self-explanatory form. Consequently, the system does not require that a data tiddler, an equation, a JSON block, or any other technical structure contain within itself all the information necessary for its human comprehension. On the contrary, it recognizes that knowledge can be distributed across different complementary layers, as long as these remain linked in an explicit and traceable manner.

Under this logic, specialized content fulfills the function of source: it preserves the data, the structure, or the expression in the form that best respects its original nature. The explanation, in contrast, fulfills an interpretive function: it contextualizes, describes, clarifies, or situates that content within a broader network of meaning. This distinction allows avoiding a frequent problem in many documentation or analysis systems, where the attempt to make everything immediately readable ends up altering the original content or mixing different levels of information within the same node.

The separation between content and explanation also allows both levels to evolve without losing coherence. A dataset can maintain its tabular form, an equation can preserve its exact notation, and a JSON block can be preserved as a technical structure, while their explanatory tiddlers can be expanded, corrected, or enriched according to new needs for comprehension, learning, or contextualization. This prevents the interpretive layer from invading the source layer and, at the same time, prevents specialized knowledge from becoming isolated or inaccessible to those who do not immediately master its technical form.

From a structural perspective, this separation is sustained through explicit relations between nodes. Specialized content is not left “alone” within the system, but rather can be connected with documentary or contextual tiddlers that explain it, describe it, or situate it within a particular logic of use. In this way, comprehension does not depend on rewriting the source content, but on integrating it into a network of nodes that grant it meaning without compromising its integrity.

Within this framework, the explanation ceases to be an informal addition and becomes a legitimate layer of the knowledge architecture. Its function is not to duplicate the technical content nor to replace it, but to accompany it and make it interpretable without destroying its original form. Thanks to this separation, the system can simultaneously sustain three demands that tend to come into tension: fidelity to the source content, human readability, and computational reusability. The resulting architecture does not require that each node be self-sufficient, but it does guarantee that each node can be incorporated within an explainable, navigable, and reversible structure.

### Relations between specialized content and context
The separation between specialized content and explanation only becomes operative if the system has clear mechanisms for linking both levels. For this reason, the architecture does not conceive of technical tiddlers and contextual tiddlers as isolated units, but rather as distinct nodes within the same knowledge network. Specialized content can preserve its own form —whether a set of tabular data, an equation, a JSON block, or any other technical structure— while its description, interpretation, or contextualization resides in other tiddlers linked in an explicit and structural manner.

These relations do not fulfill a decorative or merely auxiliary function. Their role is to prevent technical content from becoming isolated within the system. Thanks to them, a dataset can remain intact as a source and, at the same time, be linked with one or several nodes that explain its provenance, its structure, its meaning, or its analytical use. Equivalently, an equation can remain in its original notation while another tiddler situates it within a theoretical, methodological, or interpretive framework. In this way, the system does not force mixing the source with its explanation, but neither does it allow the source to remain semantically mute.

From this perspective, the relation between specialized content and context should be understood as a separate but structurally linked semantic layer. The explanation does not replace the technical content nor destructively translate it; it accompanies it as a distinct node that provides human intelligibility and inserts it within a broader network of meaning. This allows the source to remain stable, while the explanation can grow, be corrected, expanded, or refined without altering the original content. Thus, the system clearly distinguishes between what is preserved as data, equation, or technical structure, and what is added as a framework for reading, interpretation, or learning.

Furthermore, this type of relation directly strengthens the system's traceability. Not only is the content preserved in its source form, but also the set of links that explain why that content is relevant, how it is interpreted, and in what context it was used or studied. This proves especially important when the system articulates with sessions, because it allows specialized content to remain connected to the actual process of research, learning, or development. A datum can be generated or incorporated in a session, explained by another node, and then reused in subsequent contexts without losing either its technical integrity or its position within the system.

Consequently, the relations between specialized content and context constitute a necessary condition for the architecture to simultaneously sustain preservation, comprehension, and reusability. Thanks to them, the system does not require that all knowledge be immediately readable from its technical form, but it does guarantee that all content can be integrated into a broader semantic structure that makes it interpretable, traceable, and coherent with the rest of the system.

### Function of the minimum operational architecture
This layered function finds direct theoretical support in the Nested Learning paradigm proposed by Behrouz et al., who argue that a system's capacity for continual learning depends on maintaining multiple levels with distinct "context flow" and update frequencies —not on any single level's properties in isolation [7]. The architecture described below instantiates precisely this multi-frequency structure: mother nuclei update on the timescale of reconceptualization, thematic tiddlers on the timescale of study cycles, session nodes on the timescale of individual sessions, and the converter on the timescale of individual runs. Skjæveland et al.'s ecosystem framing further supports this layered design, as it demands clear interfaces among population, management, and utilization aspects [5] —each of which corresponds to a distinct layer in the present system.

The minimum operational architecture fulfills a decisive function within the system: ensuring that the growth of knowledge does not depend on spontaneous accumulation, circumstantial memory, or improvised decisions, but on a prior structure capable of receiving, ordering, and projecting each new contribution. For this reason, the nodes incorporated during a topic's development should not be understood as isolated pieces, but as internal extensions of an already existing architecture, whose layer-based organization establishes from the beginning the conditions of use, relation, and evolution of the work.

This architecture allows each new topic and each new session to be integrated into a framework that already distinguishes between different dimensions of the system. It distinguishes, for example, between the documentary and the operable, avoiding confusing the human mediation layer with the structured nodes that sustain the effective development of the topic. It also distinguishes between the stable and the evolutionary, allowing certain blocks to remain as permanent reference while others mature, are refined, or are expanded through sessions. Likewise, it separates the topic's origin from its deployment, the norm from the production, and human reading from future computational exploitation, preserving in each case the proper function of each layer.

Thanks to this internal differentiation, the minimum operational architecture does not act as a rigid template in an impoverishing sense, but as a base infrastructure that channels the growth of knowledge without blocking it. Its value does not lie in freezing the topic, but in allowing each new development to find a recognizable place within the system, to be related to prior nodes, and to remain available for subsequent processes of revision, reinterpretation, promotion, or derivation. In this regard, the architecture does not restrict the movement of work, but rather gives it continuity and direction.

This proves especially important in a system that seeks to simultaneously sustain several functions: human reference, thematic startup, epistemic control, session traceability, structured deployment, and the emergence of new elements. Without a minimum operational architecture, these functions would tend to disperse into fragmentary records or local decisions without stable memory. With it, however, the system can preserve general cohesion while admitting growth, refinement, and future operability by computational processes or AI assistance.

Consequently, the function of the minimum operational architecture should not be understood only as a matter of formal order, but as a condition of possibility for the knowledge developed within the system to maintain coherence over time. Its task is to make it possible for the topic to grow without losing structure, for sessions to produce without overflowing the framework, and for the new to be incorporated without breaking the general continuity of the process.


## Conclusions

The present technical report has described, grounded, and formalized the architecture of a structured tiddler system conceived as a non-linear knowledge infrastructure. From the analysis performed, it is possible to draw the following conclusions:

### Effective articulation between human production and computational formalization
The system resolves one of the most difficult tensions in contemporary knowledge management: allowing human thought to be recorded in an exploratory and progressive manner without preventing its subsequent formalization, validation, and computational reuse. The guiding principle that runs through the entire architecture —**the human edits a sufficient semantic interface (TiddlyWiki); the converter formalizes the canonical representation**— constitutes the single decision upon which this articulation rests. The author does not produce canonical JSON: they work within TiddlyWiki with the native tools of the environment, concentrating on the production of meaning, while the converter (tiddly-data-converter) is responsible for normalizing, complementing, and projecting the node toward an auditable, deterministic, and reversible JSONL Canon. This controlled transition avoids both premature rigidity and the disordered accumulation of information, and explicitly recognizes that the human's cognitive capacity is oriented toward the construction of knowledge, not toward technical serialization.

### Block-based architecture with clearly differentiated functions
The node's JSON schema demonstrates itself to be a well-designed structure, where each block fulfills a specific and irreducible function within the knowledge flow. The identification block (`id`, `title`) guarantees uniqueness and readability; the semantic classification block (`primary_role`, `secondary_roles`, `tags`) situates the node epistemologically and contextually; the relations block (`relations`) transforms the set of tiddlers into a navigable graph with explicit and typed links; the content block (`content`) preserves the principal semantic body without contaminating it with metadata; the metadata block (`meta`) contextualizes the node's operative state and maturity; the provenance block (`provenance`), modeled as a list of entries with explicit `actor`, guarantees traceability of each contribution to the node; and the metacognition block (`metacognition`) introduces a reflective dimension that records the confidence and ambiguity with which knowledge is sustained. The audit histories (`meta.status_history`, `metacognition.metacognition_history`) complete the traceability by recording who modified each state and when, without depending on external systems. The complementarity between these blocks allows the node to function simultaneously as a unit of cognitive work and as a computationally interpretable object.

### Graduated normative system that balances discipline and exploration
The classification of rules into three levels —obligatory, recommended, and flexible— constitutes one of the most significant architectural decisions of the system. The obligatory rules protect the node's unique identity, its minimum structure, the validity of relations, and the separation between content and meta-information, establishing an irreducible threshold of integrity. The recommended rules guide construction toward clearer and more consistent forms without blocking the development of knowledge in early phases. The flexible rules, for their part, recognize that knowledge is not born complete or stable, enabling tiddlers in an exploratory state, provisional epistemological combinations, and still partial metacognition. This graduated normative design avoids two opposite risks: turning the system into a bureaucracy that paralyzes the generation of ideas, or into a formless space where disordered accumulation prevents all traceability.

### Controlled relational vocabulary as the foundation of the knowledge graph
The system defines an explicit and bounded relational vocabulary (`uses`, `requires`, `part_of`, `defines`, `replaces`, `alternative_to`, `do_not_combine_with`) that allows precisely representing the nature of links between nodes. This decision is fundamental because it transforms the network of tiddlers into a semantic graph with computationally exploitable properties: dependencies, hierarchies, alternatives, and incompatibilities are formalized and can be traversed, analyzed, and validated by both people and automated systems. The rule that requires evaluating whether a new type is truly necessary before extending the vocabulary protects the system against semantic fragmentation and the ad hoc growth of relational categories.

### Treatment of specialized content without loss of integrity
The architecture coherently resolves the problem of incorporating technically dense content —tabular data, JSON structures, equations in KaTeX notation, code, data dictionaries— without forcing an artificial homogenization. The principle of separation between source content and explanation layer allows the data, the equation, or the technical structure to be preserved intact in their original form, while their interpretation and contextualization is modeled in structurally linked tiddlers. This strategy simultaneously satisfies three demands that habitually come into tension: fidelity to the source content, human readability, and computational reusability.

### Minimum operational tiddlers as the topic's base infrastructure
The definition of a minimum set of operational tiddlers, organized by layers —general orientation, structural nuclei, thematic blocks, normative principles, and specific elements—, offers a stable infrastructure for the development of any topic without depending on spontaneous accumulation. This organization guarantees that each work session can be integrated into a pre-existing framework, produce new nodes, and expand the knowledge network without disrupting the general architecture. The selective incorporation of documentary tiddlers in markdown as a cognitive mediation layer —reserved exclusively for mother blocks or strategic nodes— complements the structure without becoming a second source of truth nor a redundant duplication of the system.

### Epistemological honesty as a transversal principle
Throughout the entire architecture, a commitment to epistemological honesty manifests that transcends the technical. The system not only distinguishes between fact, hypothesis, interpretation, intuition, and procedure, but also explicitly records the provenance of knowledge through a list of contributions with identified actor (human, artificial, external), the method of obtainment (observation, inference, generation, estimation, compilation), and the degree of certainty and ambiguity with which each assertion is sustained. The provenance model as a list allows accumulating multiple contributions on the same node —for example, a human draft followed by an AI-assisted revision— without one erasing the other, thus preserving the complete chain of authorship. This multi-dimensionality prevents contents in formation from being treated as consolidated knowledge, AI-assisted productions from being confused with primary sources, or weak intuitions from being presented as stabilized certainties. In a context where collaboration with generative models is increasingly frequent, this capacity for epistemological discrimination is not only desirable, but indispensable.

### Viability for multiple contexts of use
The system's design demonstrates viability for operating in multiple simultaneous contexts: progressive human learning, knowledge graph construction, statistical analysis, artificial intelligence, data pipelines, Markov governance models, and audit processes. The `intended_use` field of the `meta` block, together with the converter's capability to generate derived profiles without altering the Canon, allows the same body of knowledge to serve diverse purposes without fragmenting into parallel systems nor losing its single source of truth.

### Final consideration
The structured tiddler system described in this report does not constitute a simple note storage format, but rather a complete epistemological architecture that articulates human production of meaning, structural discipline, computational traceability, and metacognitive reflexivity within a single model. Its guiding principle —**the human edits a sufficient semantic interface (TiddlyWiki); the converter formalizes the canonical representation**— constitutes the single decision upon which the entire architecture rests, and explicitly resolves the tension between the limited capabilities of the human author and the demands of a computationally rigorous system. Its strength lies in having found an operative balance between the necessary rigidity to sustain technical coherence and the indispensable flexibility to accompany the actual process of knowledge construction. To the extent that contemporary intellectual work environments increasingly demand the capacity to produce knowledge that is simultaneously comprehensible by people, traceable by systems, and reusable by artificial intelligence, the architecture formalized herein offers a structurally grounded response to that triple demand.
